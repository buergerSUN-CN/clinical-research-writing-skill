# Clinical tables, figures, and legends

Make every display item interpretable when separated from the manuscript.

## Self-containment {#self-containment}

Name the clinical endpoint, time point, groups or strata, cohort/analysis population, and relevant
estimator. Expand abbreviations used in that item. Re-define scales and cutoffs when their meaning is
not obvious from the row or axis.

## Titles {#titles}

- Describe what the item shows; do not put a conclusion or P value in the title.
- Name the population and time point when they affect interpretation.
- Distinguish intention-to-treat, per-protocol, and safety populations.
- Prefer the clinical endpoint over the statistical object: identify the outcome rather than writing
  only `odds-ratio plot` or `score distribution`.

## Figure legends {#legends}

- Start with the display type, endpoint, groups, and population.
- Decode panels in order and explain symbols, lines, shading, and error bars.
- State the relevant test or effect estimate with uncertainty when it is needed to interpret the figure.
- Map each panel to its cohort or subgroup; do not leave panel letters unexplained.
- Keep mechanisms in Discussion and acquisition detail in Methods.

## Forest and subgroup plots {#forest}

- Label both directions of benefit and the null line in clinical language.
- Show effect estimates and confidence intervals, including intervals crossing the null.
- Report interaction tests separately from within-subgroup estimates.
- State prespecified/post hoc status and multiplicity limits.
- Keep adjustment sets identical to the corresponding Methods and table footnotes.

## Diagnostic and ROC plots {#diagnostic}

- Report AUC with uncertainty and comparison test when relevant.
- State how a threshold was selected and give sensitivity/specificity at that threshold only if present
  in the source analysis.
- Identify the index test, reference standard, and unit of analysis.
- Do not promote discrimination into clinical utility without decision-impact evidence.

## Time-to-event and ordinal outcomes {#outcomes}

- Kaplan-Meier figures include numbers at risk, time units, censoring notation, and uncertainty bands.
- Ordinal outcomes show the full distribution when the analysis uses a shift model; define the direction
  of better outcome and missing/death handling.
- State rounding when displayed percentages may not sum to 100%.

## Representative cases {#cases}

- Provide only source-supported age/sex/context, timing, anatomy, modality, and relevant clinical
  outcome.
- Walk panels in order and match every arrow or marker to a described finding.
- Avoid cherry-picked confirmation; label the case as illustrative rather than representative unless
  the sampling method supports that word.

## Table footnotes {#footnotes}

Use a consistent order:

1. Data formats and units.
2. Endpoint, scale, cutoff, reference category, and analysis-population definitions.
3. Adjustment, missing-data, multiplicity, and estimator notes.
4. Alphabetized abbreviation and symbol definitions.

Show exact denominators for variables with missing data. Use dashes or `NA` for inapplicable cells,
not zero or blanks.

## Table 1 {#table1}

- Group variables into clinically meaningful blocks and put units/time windows in row labels.
- Show group denominators in column headers.
- For matched or weighted cohorts, report before/after balance and the prespecified balance metric.
- Do not interpret baseline significance tests as proof of comparability.

## Flow diagrams {#flow}

Keep screened, excluded with reasons, allocated, treated, followed, and analyzed counts auditable.
Mirror eligibility definitions without adding criteria absent from Methods.

## Prose-display division {#division}

- Tables carry the audit trail; prose carries the principal clinical result and decision-relevant
  numbers.
- Do not duplicate every table cell in Results.
- Reconcile every number and label across text, tables, figures, and supplements before delivery.
