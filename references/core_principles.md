# Core clinical-writing principles

Use these rules for every specialty and study design.

## Clinical subject {#clinical-subject}

- Put the population, endpoint, treatment choice, diagnostic decision, prognosis, or workflow in
  grammatical control of the paper.
- Introduce mechanism only when it explains an observed clinical finding or a management question.
- Replace claims about technical novelty with the clinical uncertainty the technique could resolve.

## Evidence boundary {#evidence-boundary}

- Build a fact ledger before rewriting: population, time window, sample size, groups, endpoints,
  effect estimates, uncertainty, missingness, methods, and citations.
- Label every proposed addition as `source fact`, `source-stated interpretation`, `inference`, or
  `author query`. Draft the first two by default. Use an inference only when the task authorizes
  interpretation and signal its status; never convert it into a reported fact.
- Preserve denominators, units, direction, confidence intervals, P values, and endpoint definitions.
- Preserve semantic labels exactly: `studies` must not become `records`; an interval must not be called
  a 95% CI unless the source identifies it as one; `associated` must not become `predicted` casually.
- Do not convert association into causation, exploratory analyses into confirmation, or a secondary
  endpoint into the study's principal conclusion.
- When only an abstract or fact sheet is available, do not manufacture conventional background,
  mechanisms, unreported limitations, management advice, or recommended next steps to make a section
  look complete. Omit them or raise an author query.

## Section boundary {#section-boundary}

- Introduction: establish burden or standard care, unresolved clinical uncertainty, and the study aim.
- Methods: state what was done with enough precision to reproduce or audit it.
- Results: report the cohort and findings without explanation, advocacy, or speculative hedging.
- Discussion: interpret the principal findings, compare evidence, explain plausible mechanisms,
  state clinical meaning, and delimit inference.
- Conclusion: answer the stated aim at the same or lower claim strength than the Discussion.

## Numeric reporting {#numeric-reporting}

- Attach every rate to a recoverable denominator when available.
- Pair adjusted estimates with the named effect measure and uncertainty interval.
- Keep descriptive statistics attached to dispersion and time points.
- State null findings plainly; do not rescue them with editorial adjectives.

## Claim ladder {#claim-ladder}

Use the highest defensible rung, never a higher one:

1. Descriptive: `was observed` or `occurred`.
2. Associational: `was associated with` or `independently predicted` when the model supports it.
3. Diagnostic/prognostic: `showed discrimination`, `improved classification`, or `may aid` when
   validation and reference-standard conditions support the statement.
4. Comparative causal: reserve treatment-effect language for a design that supports it.
5. Practice-changing: reserve recommendations for adequately powered randomized evidence and align
   them with the prespecified endpoint and safety findings.

## Revision discipline {#revision-discipline}

- Rewrite the smallest unit that fixes the problem.
- Preserve a defensible author-specific cadence; do not smooth every sentence to the same length.
- Prefer explicit clinical nouns and verbs over abstract evaluative language.
- Record unresolved factual gaps as author queries.
