# Figures, Tables & Legends — Clinical Writing Guide

Distilled from 21 exemplar papers — a Nanjing neurovascular/stroke + neuroimaging group plus landmark multicentre RCTs (NEJM/Lancet/JAMA). Verbatim exemplars tagged with paperNN.

How the exemplars write display items so they are self-contained and guide clinical interpretation. This area was deliberately deep-studied this round; use it when writing/rewriting figure legends, figure & table titles, and table footnotes.

## Figure legends / captions

**Moves:**
- Write the legend as a self-contained two-part unit: a short declarative TITLE sentence/noun phrase naming WHAT the panel shows (the display type + endpoint + comparison groups + analysis population), then an interpretation/decoding body that states HOW to read it (panel-by-panel key tied to (A)(B)... letters, the quantitative readout, or the take-home). The legend must be fully readable when the figure is extracted alone (paper03 Fig 1/2/3; paper04 Fig 1/2/3; paper06 Fig 1/2 bold-lead clinical titles; paper22 Fig 1; paper26 Fig 2/3).
- Frame the TITLE around the patient + decision point, not the statistical object: encode endpoint + timepoint + population + the analysis-population/eligibility window so a reader scanning only exhibits knows the denominator and time anchor (paper20 'Figure 1. Distribution of the Modified Rankin Scale Scores at 1 Year (Intention-to-Treat Population)'; paper21 Fig 2 'within 24 Hours after Symptom Onset'; paper22 Fig 1/2 append '(Intention-to-Treat Population)'/'(Primary Outcome)'; paper26 Fig 2/3 title by endpoint, not 'OR plot'). Name the analysis population in EVERY RCT figure title (ITT vs safety vs per-protocol) as a repeated framing device (paper24 'intention-to-treat population'; paper25 'in the Intention-to-Treat Population').
- Embed the statistical verdict directly in the legend, not just in the body: name the test inline and give the P value ('P<0.001, log-rank test'; 'The P value is 0.17 for log-rank testing between the 2 groups'), and for diagnostic curves put the headline metric with its 95% CI in parentheses ('area under the curve=0.774'; 'AUC is 0.72 (95% CI: 0.62-0.83; P < 0.01)'). The legend tells the reader what the figure MEANS (paper02 Fig 2; paper03 Fig 2; paper04 Figs 1-3; paper06 Fig 1/2; paper12 Fig 3; paper14 Fig 5).
- Restate the single headline EFFECT ESTIMATE with its 95% CI (and the test/cut-off) inside the legend so the figure both shows and tells (paper14 ROC restates AUC/sens/spec + dAUC test; paper20 Fig 1 restates common OR, Fig 2 restates HR; paper21 Fig 2 restates the generalized OR; paper26 Fig 3 restates OR + estimation method + direction). Do NOT report a bare effect estimate without its CI and the named test/cut-off that produced it.
- For a comparison figure, pair the positive finding with the explicit NULL finding so the figure cannot be over-read, carrying the pairwise contrast a table's single omnibus P cannot ('which access loses'); flag the null explicitly ('No significant differences were found... (all P > 0.05)'; paper12 Fig 4 'No significant difference was found in the proportion of patients with the occurrence of hemorrhagic transformation between these two groups (40.9% vs 59.4%; p = 0.130)') (paper01 Fig 1; paper12 Fig 4). [CANONICAL HOME for the null-flag doctrine; also enforced as a pitfall.]
- Restate cohort, denominator, group definitions and strata cut-offs INSIDE the legend: give the n ('among 56 patients with restenosis') and re-define risk strata by their cut-offs in the caption ('high-risk group (score 3-6)' / 'low-risk group (score 0-2)'; 'Stratified based on BMI >= 24.5 kg/m2 and BMI <24.5 kg/m2') (paper03 Fig 1/2; paper04 Fig 2).
- Repeat the diagnostic cut-off and its operating characteristics in the legend so a clinician gets the decision rule without the body: state AUC (with 95% CI and P), the optimal cut-off, and sensitivity/specificity at that cut-off (paper04 Fig 1; paper14 Fig 5).
- CANONICAL HOME for the scale-regloss doctrine (range + direction-of-meaning), referenced from pitfalls, table_footnotes, and display_item_strategy rather than repeated there: re-gloss every clinical scale's range AND direction-of-meaning inside the legend each time it appears, even when defined in Methods, treating each display item as self-contained ('Scores on the modified Rankin scale range from 0 to 6, with higher scores indicating greater disability'; 'ASPECTS values range from 0 to 10, with lower values indicating larger infarction') (paper20; paper21 Fig 1; paper22 Fig 2; paper25 Fig 1 appends ranges to abbreviation expansions). Always spell out direction-of-effect ('higher = worse') in plain clinical terms; a raw scale number is uninterpretable without it.
- Translate the ordinal mRS endpoint into a level-by-level bedside disability gradient inside the legend (anchor 0 through death at 6), turning a stacked bar into a locatable disability spectrum ('a score of 3, moderate disability (patients require some help but are able to walk unassisted)... and a score of 6, death') (paper20 Fig 1; paper21 Fig 2; paper22 Fig 1; paper24 Fig 2). [Specialization of the scale-regloss doctrine for the ordinal mRS.]
- Make multi-panel legends map each panel letter to its own subgroup/cohort AND its own test/P (or its own grade), so effect-modification or contrast is legible from the figure alone (paper04 Fig 3 per-BMI-stratum log-rank P; paper06 Fig 2 per-panel '(A, P=0.86, log-rank test)'; paper26 Fig 2 'A, all patients; B, propensity score matching data set'; paper01 Fig 2 panels a-h).
- DEFINE the graphical uncertainty element explicitly so a band is not misread as an IQR or range ('The shading represents the 95% CI.') (paper04 Figs 2-3; paper06 both figures).
- Embed the take-home CLAIM in the legend with an explicit what-NOT-to-over-read restraint cue: fence subgroup/secondary findings ('A significant positive correlation... was observed only in the vertebrobasilar subgroup'; the word 'only' fences the claim), and add the not-powered/no-multiplicity/post-hoc guardrail on every subgroup/forest figure ('The trial was not powered... for a definitive analysis of subgroups'; '*Post-hoc analysis') (paper08 Fig 3; paper21 Fig 3; paper22 Fig 2; paper24 Fig 3; paper25 bakes 'Prespecified' into the title).
- CANONICAL HOME for the forest-plot direction-anchor doctrine (referenced from pitfalls rather than restated): orient forest/subgroup plots for bedside reading with plain-language direction anchors flanking the line of no effect ('Control Better | Endovascular Thrombectomy Better'; 'Favors control | Favors tirofiban'), a 'P for interaction' column, and an off-scale-arrow note where CIs run out of frame ('The arrow indicates that the confidence interval extends outside the graphed area') (paper20 Fig 3; paper21 Fig 3; paper22 Fig 2; paper23 Fig 3; paper24 Fig 3; paper25 Fig 2). Do NOT use bare statistical axis labels (OR, RR) when plain-language directional labels serve the clinician.
- List the EXACT adjustment covariate set inline in subgroup/forest legends (in the same canonical order across display items, and identical to the matching table footnote), flagging any model-specific exclusion/collinearity drop, so the adjusted estimate is interpretable without Methods (paper19 Fig 3; paper20 Fig 3; paper23 Fig 3 collinearity caveat; paper26 Fig 3 full covariate list).
- Add an INTERPRETIVE INSTRUCTION to pre-empt a reviewer objection for methods-credibility figures ('The substantial overlap between groups indicates a large area of common support for the eligible participants.'; 'Note that the infarct area shown on follow-up DWI was significantly smaller than that shown on baseline...') (paper05 Fig 3; paper11 Fig 2).
- Open every representative-case legend with a one-line clinical vignette in a fixed order (age + sex + clinical context + interval since procedure + exact vessel/segment), walk it panel-by-panel naming modality + technique + finding + on-image annotation in parentheses ('c, arrowhead'; 'white arrow'; 'yellow line'), embed concrete per-panel quantities (exact infarction volume + ASPECTS with scored regions named; perfusion volumes + HIR), and CLOSE on the hard decision-relevant outcome - always the 90-day/3-month mRS ('The mRS score was 3 at 3 months') (paper09 Fig 3-4; paper11 Fig 2; paper12 Fig 1-2; paper13 Fig 3-4; paper14 Fig 4). NOTE paper09 is the corpus's IMAGING-TECHNIQUE exemplar: its representative-case figures illustrate image quality / diagnostic confidence (its prespecified PRIMARY endpoint, scored on a 5-point Likert scale and sized by a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank power calculation), with diagnostic accuracy SECONDARY - so its legends still close on the patient context and the DSA arbiter, not on an accuracy statistic.
- Pair a positive and a negative case on an identical modality sequence so prognosis/diagnosis is read by contrast, and deliberately pair opposite ERROR DIRECTIONS for a diagnostic figure (one underestimation case + one overestimation case), using 'confirms' for the arbiter panel ('Digital subtraction angiography confirms...') (paper09 Fig 3 underestimation vs Fig 4 overestimation; paper12 good Fig 1 vs poor Fig 2; paper13 FVH+ Fig 3 vs FVH- Fig 4). Do NOT cherry-pick only confirmatory cases.
- For representative-case / grading figures, RESTATE the ordinal grading scheme inline and map each panel letter to its grade with the verbal anchor re-quoted, so the figure is self-contained against the reference standard ('grade 2, the lesion is clearly delineated as a hypointense region without DWI reference (a, b); grade 1: ...') (paper07 Fig 2).
- For multi-panel anatomy/imaging figures, use a PANEL-MAPPING title sentence assigning each panel range to a disease subtype/grade, then annotate ONLY the panels that contain a discrete lesion (leave by-definition-empty panels unmarked) (paper05 Fig 1 annotates only aneurysm panels I-L, leaving angiogram-negative panels A-H unmarked).
- For chart legends, embed the headline numbers and a PLAIN-LANGUAGE reading guide (what each axis/bar means) plus a tiered significance-marker key ('* represent p < 0.05; ** represent p < 0.01; *** represent p < 0.001'); for boxplots restate medians + p inline (paper07 Fig 4/5; paper14 Fig 3).
- Narrate a method-schematic legend conceptually (what is computed from what), naming the software once, for figures that explain a calculation (paper14 Fig 1 NWU quantification).
- For Kaplan-Meier figures include a 'No. at risk' table by arm and timepoint beneath the curves, exposing the shrinking denominators (paper20 Fig 2).
- Disclose per-arm missing data inside the figure legend with exact counts and the imputation/handling rule, so every bar's denominator is reconstructable ('*Data were missing for two of 1153 participants in the endovascular treatment group'; 'Data were missing for one patient... which were imputed using the 30-day score') (paper23 Fig 2; paper24 Fig 2; paper25 Fig 2).
- Add a rounding note when stacked-bar percentages do not sum to 100 ('Percentages may not total 100 because of rounding.') (paper22 Fig 1; paper24 Fig 2).
- Write CONSORT/flow-diagram legends as a terse operational ledger: a one-line enrolment caption plus the abbreviation glossary; put the full quantitative eligibility filter and itemized counted exclusion reasons into the diagram boxes/footnote, keeping Methods prose clean ('191 excluded / 139 did not meet inclusion or met exclusion criteria / 47 time from stroke onset to randomisation >6 h...'; footnote 'Multimodal CT or MRI criteria: ischemic penumbra, more than 10 mL; infarct core, less than 70 mL; and mismatch ratio, more than 1.2.') (paper02 Fig 1; paper07 Fig 1; paper24 Fig 1; paper25 Fig 1).
- CANONICAL HOME for the per-item abbreviation-key doctrine (referenced from table_footnotes and display_item_strategy rather than restated): give every figure its own complete, self-contained abbreviation key as the LAST element, defining ONLY that figure's own acronyms - including graphic-only ones never spelled in the legend prose - even when a master abbreviation box exists, so each item is portable when extracted (paper02 Fig 1; paper04 Figs 1-2; paper05 Fig 2 expands graphic-only EVT; paper09; paper13; paper19; paper26 Figs 1-3). PROVISIONAL / LOW-N punctuation house-split (each drawn from only 1-2 papers per journal in a ~21-paper sample; verify against the target journal's CURRENT author instructions rather than treating as settled): figure-legend keys tend to use 'X indicates Y; Z, W' (first term 'indicates', rest comma-chained), distinct from the table-footnote pure comma-list 'X, Y; Z, W' (paper13); JAHA / Translational Stroke Research legends tend to use comma-style 'term, expansion;' runs - match whichever the target journal actually uses, but expand exhaustively either way.
- Keep legends non-mechanistic and the test method OUT of the legend: describe cohort, timepoints, rates, the metric/verdict; leave 'why' (mechanism/physics) entirely to the Discussion and the statistical TEST name to Methods; never editorialize or over-claim ('was used to evaluate the predictive ability... (area under the curve=0.774)' states metric not method; paper09 keeps detector physics out of legends entirely) (paper02 Fig 2; paper03 figures-block convention; paper09; paper13).
- Offload the full actuarial readout into the legend for survival curves: list group-wise restenosis-free/cumulative rates at each timepoint right in the caption so the quotable numbers travel with the curve (paper03 Fig 2). Offload the honest counter-example but keep it locatable, never hidden ('The only discordant case on UHR PCD-CTA is shown in Figure S2') (paper09).
- For an illustrative/salvage procedural case figure, write the legend as a chronological clinical narrative bound to lettered panels (failure -> conversion -> complication -> success -> clean safety endpoint) and specify device makes/models and exact dimensions inline so an operator can reproduce the steps (paper01 Fig 2).

**Pitfalls:**
- Do NOT let a single omnibus P in the figure stand in for the pairwise contrast the reader actually needs; the figure legend is where the 'which group differs' interpretation belongs (paper01 routes pairwise interpretation into the Fig 1 legend because the table carries only one omnibus P per row).
- NULL-FLAG (see figure_legends.moves for the canonical statement): do NOT report only the positive contrast in a comparison-figure legend; omitting the explicit null ('No significant differences were found... (all P > 0.05)') invites over-reading (paper01 Fig 1; paper12 Fig 4 keeps the null HT row).
- Do NOT report a bare effect estimate in the legend without its 95% CI and the named test/cut-off that produced it, and do NOT compute/recompute the statistic in the legend; state the metric/verdict, not the method (paper02 Fig 2 'was used to evaluate... (area under the curve=0.774)'; counter-modeled on paper14, paper20, paper21).
- Do NOT force every panel under one P value when the design is stratified; each effect-modification panel needs its OWN test and P (paper04 Fig 3 gives a separate log-rank P per BMI stratum; paper06 Fig 2 per-panel test).
- Do NOT leave a shaded band ambiguous; always say what it is ('The shading represents 95% CI.') - omitting this lets a reader mistake the band for an IQR or data range (paper06).
- Do NOT title a figure by its variable or statistical object alone ('mRS distribution', 'odds ratio plot'); it must state in WHOM and at what TIMEPOINT, and name the patient endpoint not the statistical machinery (paper20 'Distribution... at 1 Year (Intention-to-Treat Population)'; paper24; paper26 Fig 2/3).
- ABBREVIATION-KEY (see figure_legends.moves for the canonical statement): do NOT assume the reader will carry abbreviations from the body into the figure; expand them in-legend even when the audience would 'know' them, re-define per figure, and decode every symbol used ANYWHERE in the figure (paper04 re-defines IIS in Fig 1 and Fig 2; paper05 expands graphic-only EVT in Fig 2).
- SCALE-REGLOSS (see figure_legends.moves for the canonical statement): do NOT cross-reference Methods for a scale's range/direction or assume a Methods-defined scale needs no re-glossing; repeat the primer (range + direction) in the legend each appearance because a clinician may read the figure in isolation (paper21 repeats mRS/ASPECTS/NIHSS verbatim; paper22 re-defines NIHSS in Fig 2).
- Do NOT present a subgroup/forest figure as confirmatory; always pair it with the underpowered/multiplicity/post-hoc caveat and show CIs that cross 1 rather than suppressing them (paper21 'no conclusions can be drawn'; paper19; paper22; paper24).
- FOREST-DIRECTION (see figure_legends.moves for the canonical statement): do NOT leave forest-plot direction to the reader's inference; label which side favours which arm and flag off-scale CIs (paper22 'The arrow indicates that the confidence interval extends outside the graphed area').
- Do NOT let the adjustment set drift between the figure legend and the table footnote, and do NOT leave a forest plot's effect measure, comparison direction, or covariate set implicit - define them inside the legend (paper19; paper26 Fig 3).
- Do NOT collapse an ordinal endpoint to a single dichotomized bar when the full disability spectrum (including death/mRS 6) is the clinically informative view (paper19 Fig 2; paper20 Fig 1; paper21 Fig 2).
- Do NOT let a representative-case legend end on the image finding; it must close on the patient outcome (mRS) - a legend that stops at 'recanalization mTICI 3' without the 90-day mRS breaks the fixed template (paper11, paper12, paper13, paper14). For paper09 (imaging-technique exemplar), likewise do NOT promote a SECONDARY accuracy statistic into a representative-case legend's headline - its primary endpoint was image quality / diagnostic confidence, and its case legends close on patient context + the DSA arbiter.
- Do NOT annotate panels/arrows that by definition have nothing to point at (paper05 marks arrows only on aneurysm panels I-L, leaving angiogram-negative panels A-H unmarked).
- Do NOT put mechanism, physics, or the statistical TEST name into a legend; that belongs in Discussion/Methods respectively (paper03 convention; paper09 keeps cadmium-telluride/photon-counting physics in main text only; paper13).
- Do NOT hide missingness; itemize per-arm counts and the handling rule in the legend so each bar's denominator is auditable (paper23, paper24, paper25).
- PRESERVE source artifacts verbatim when quoting (do not silently 'fix' them): paper01 Figure 2 legend contains a duplicated '(e)' panel (a real published typographic defect); paper12 Fig 4 source legend skips (e) and jumps (d) to (f) (an extraction artifact to repair consciously, not reproduce). Watch and flag these, do not propagate the error into a clean exemplar.
- Project calibration (CLAUDE.md): the Lancet-IPD-style legends (paper23) sit at the statistically dense end (full adjustment set, p-sidedness, per-cell missingness, collinearity exclusions). Prefer SHORTER, concept-focused legends with confident main text; borrow the decision-relevant elements (endpoint definition, cut-off, named classification, direction anchors, adjustment intent, population tag) but NOT paper23's defensive density. [CANONICAL HOME for the 'prefer-shorter / paper23 calibration' note; cross-referenced from table_footnotes and display_item_strategy.]

## Figure & table titles

**Moves:**
- Make the title a self-contained descriptor of WHAT the table shows, carrying the contrast and the denominator, framed as evidence to be judged, not a result or a P value - the table is evidence, not a conclusion (paper01 Tables 1-2; paper02 Tables 1-2; paper26 Tables 1-2 plain declarative noun phrases, interpretation lives in footnotes).
- Name the table by its clinical ROLE so the role is obvious: Table 1 = who the patients were / comparability ('Baseline Patient Characteristics'; 'Baseline Characteristics of the Patients'); the outcome table = the answer ('Trial Outcomes According to Assigned Treatment'; 'Adjusted Association of Effectiveness and Safety Outcomes with EVT'; 'Efficacy and Safety Outcomes') (paper01 Table 1/2; paper19 Table 1/2; paper20 Table 1/2; paper21 Table 1/2).
- Write the title as a full clinical descriptor that pins BOTH content AND analysis population (and timepoint where relevant), so the denominator and endpoint window never need to be inferred; name the analysis population in EVERY RCT table title and keep the three populations distinct (efficacy tables tagged intention-to-treat, safety table tagged safety population) (paper20; paper22 Table 2; paper23 Table 2/3; paper24 'Table 2 ...intention-to-treat population' vs 'Table 3 ...safety population'; paper25 Table 1/2).
- Encode the ENDPOINT and TIME STRUCTURE in the title rather than leaving them to a column header, so the display item is self-contained and decision-oriented (paper06 Table 2 'Probabilities of Ipsilateral Ischemic Stroke ... at Various Time Points'; paper12 Table 4 'Diagnostic performance ... in predicting good outcomes at 90 days').
- Name the COMPARISON AXIS and the ANALYTIC STATE explicitly ('according to treatment modality' + 'before and after propensity score matching'; 'with and without [outcome]'; 'Versus'), so the reader knows what is contrasted and at which stage before reading rows (paper02 Table 1; paper08 Table 1/2; paper09 Table 2 'Versus'; paper13 Table 2/3; paper05 Table 1 'Before and After Propensity Score Matching').
- For a method-heavy / OR / effect table, write the title as a full sentence encoding METHOD + ENDPOINT (with acronym defined in-place) + COHORT + DATA SOURCE, and state the EXACT estimand and the denominator (n) so the analytic sample is explicit and the matched-analysis n is not confused with the regression n (paper02 Table 2 '...(n=395)'; paper04 Table 2 method+endpoint(IIS)+cohort+CMOSS source; paper08 Table 3 '...(n = 419)').
- Make the title state the STRATIFIER - what the rows/columns are split by - so the reader knows the comparison before reading cells; carry the cut-off/stratifier into the title for a dichotomized analysis ('stratified by reperfusion status'; 'stratified according to BMI levels'; 'Dichotomized analysis ... based on post-treatment DWI ASPECTS') (paper04 Table 1; paper12 Table 5; paper14 Table 1).
- Name BOTH what a bedside-instrument table predicts AND how it was built ('the related restenosis rate' + 'constructed with the regression coefficient-based scoring') (paper03 Table 1).
- Name BOTH the analytic/methodological LAYER and the content: pair the dataset with its balance/analytic framing ('Baseline Characteristics and Propensity Score Matching Measures'), and title outcome tables by the prespecified OUTCOME HIERARCHY so the title mirrors the Abstract/Results ('Primary and Secondary Outcomes and Safety Outcomes') (paper26 Table 1/2).
- Keep the title outcome- and decision-forward, not method-forward (name the clinical endpoint/characteristic, not the statistical machinery), and signal when a baseline table mixes clinical AND procedural characteristics so the procedural audience is cued ('Baseline characteristics and clinical procedural characteristics'; 'Baseline Patient Characteristics and Procedural Characteristics') (paper14 Table 3; paper20 Table 2; paper24 Table 1; paper25 Table 1).
- A terse noun-phrase title is acceptable ONLY for a generic baseline table or when the journal's column headers already carry the stratifier and timepoint; reserve it for low-information tables, and make the title carry the comparison + conditioning variable when the table carries the key clinical contrast (paper07 Table 1 'Patient characteristics' vs Table 2 'The difference in CEST parameters between patients with good or poor 90-day prognosis at different saturation powers').
- For the imaging-TECHNIQUE genre (paper09), title the evaluation table by its head-to-head image-quality/diagnostic-confidence comparison ('... on UHR PCD-CTA Versus Simulated EID-CTA'), reflecting that image quality / diagnostic confidence - not diagnostic accuracy - was the prespecified PRIMARY endpoint; do not promote a secondary accuracy table to the lead exhibit (paper09 Table 2).

## Table footnotes

**Conventions:**
- Default the footnote to a FIXED three-part scaffold: (1) a data-format sentence stating the default summary statistic with an exception clause if needed ('Data are reported as median (interquartile range [IQR]) unless otherwise indicated'), (2) ONE alphabetized run-on sentence expanding EVERY abbreviation the table uses, (3) a symbol key tying any asterisk/superscript to its meaning. Re-expand every abbreviation even if the body text or another table already defined it, expanding ONLY what THIS table contains, so each table is independently legible - this is the table-footnote application of the per-item abbreviation-key doctrine whose canonical statement lives in figure_legends.moves (paper01 both tables; paper05/paper06; paper09; paper11; paper12; paper13; paper26 Table 1). Tailor the data-format line to the table's actual content (add 'or mean+/-SD' only when a continuous row exists) (paper05 Table 1 has Age row vs Table 2 categorical-only).
- Keep statistical methods, TEST names, and cut-offs OUT of the footnote where house style allows - those live in Methods; table rows use the bare label and silently inherit the Methods-defined cut-off/instrument range; let footnote length scale to the number of NEW abbreviations introduced, not table size (paper01 figures-block convention; paper13 Table 5 footnote is leanest, defining only 'FVH'). EXCEPTION for broad-audience / RCT tables: name the comparison test and modality split/threshold in the baseline-table footnote so comparability is judgeable (paper19 'Categorical variables were compared using the chi2 test or Fisher exact test... Continuous variables were compared using the Mann-Whitney U test').
- Self-label each continuous-variable ROW inline with its summary statistic AND clinical unit ('median (IQR), min' / 'mean (SD), years' / 'mg/L'), and encode summary statistic + units in the ROW LABEL rather than the footnote where the journal allows, so the distributional assumption and unit are read at the point of the cell ('Age (years), mean +/- SD'; 'Male gender, n (%)') (paper01 Table 2; paper02 Table 1; paper13).
- When the table must stand alone for a wider/RCT audience, put the OPERATIONAL DEFINITION of the exposure/endpoint into the footnote and re-define every clinical SCALE's range AND direction-of-meaning each table (with a citation where a scale carries a formal grading) - this is the table-footnote application of the scale-regloss doctrine whose canonical statement lives in figure_legends.moves - so a number is interpretable without Methods ('BMI is the weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared'; 'Scores on the modified Rankin scale of functional disability range from 0 (no disability) to 6 (death)'; 'Scores on the NIHSS range from 0 to 42, with higher scores indicating greater neurologic deficits') (paper04 Tables 1-2; paper20 Table 1; paper21 Table 1; paper22 Table 1/2; paper24 Table 1; paper25 Table 1).
- Flag statistical significance with a symbol on the significant row/cell and decode it in the footnote ('*Statistically significant.') rather than bolding; tell the reader exactly what the mark means. NOTE the contrasting convention: paper01 uses NO asterisk/dagger system and one omnibus P per row, read directly from the numeric P column (paper04 Table 1; paper05/paper06; paper01).
- State the exact ADJUSTMENT covariate set in a footnote (quoted in full, identical wording/order across the figure legend and the efficacy/safety table footnotes), name the estimator per outcome row (generalized OR for ordinal mRS shift; HR for death; mean difference for volume/NIHSS; RR for binary; ARR), and add an effect-metric direction rule so a ratio is interpretable - so 'adjusted' is confirmable and the same model is verifiable everywhere (paper19 Table 2 = Fig 3 set; paper20 Table 2; paper21 Table 2 master footnote; paper22 Table 2; paper23 Table 2/3; paper24 Table 2/3; paper26 Table 2 footnotes * and †). Disambiguate different estimands by symbol (a distinct marker flags that beta came from a linear model while ORs came from logistic) (paper26 Table 2 footnote ‖).
- Pin the ENDPOINT + cut-off identically every time and define it operationally next to the number, naming the instrument/classification (often with a citation) and the adjudicating body, without re-stating the full operational definition where the cut-off lives once in Methods - the footnote then only decodes the acronym ('Symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage was defined according to the Heidelberg bleeding classification...15'; 'mTICI modified Thrombolysis in Cerebral Infarction') (paper08 Table 2; paper21 Table 2 sICH; paper22 Table 2 sICH adjudicator; paper23 Table 3 Heidelberg; paper14 mRS > 2; paper19 mRS 0-3).
- Disclose MISSINGNESS numerically, per variable and per dataset/arm, with exact denominators (and reconcile it with clinically plausible causes), and state the prespecified death-handling rule (worst score assigned for deaths) - account for EVERY missing patient ('Data were missing for 2 of 1153 participants...'; 'recanalization data unavailable because of serious illness or death'; 'The worst possible score was assigned for patients who had died, in accordance with the statistical analysis plan'); embed per-cell n inline where data are missing ('n = 157') and reprint denominators in every column header (paper04 Table 1 LDL row; paper05/paper06/paper07/paper08 column-header denominators; paper21 Table 2; paper22 Table 2; paper24 Table 2; paper26 Table 1/2).
- RESTATE the decision cut-off for any balance metric in the footnote, redundant with Methods on purpose so the threshold travels with the table ('SMD <= 0.1 in the absolute value was considered significantly balanced'); quantify baseline balance in-table with SMD rather than baseline P values (paper08 Table 1/2; paper26 Table 1).
- Pre-empt over-interpretation in the footnote: state the multiplicity caveat ('not adjusted for multiple comparisons... may not be used for hypothesis testing') and the underpowered-subgroup caveat, and flag what NOT to conclude when a statistical method switches (e.g., at zero events) ('Unadjusted risk difference was reported; relative risk was not measurable since the symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage rate was 0 in the tirofiban group') (paper20; paper21 Table 2; paper24 Table 2; paper25 Table 2 footnote j).
- Disambiguate p-value SIDEDNESS in non-inferiority designs (one-sided primary non-inferiority vs two-sided superiority) so a bare p column becomes a correctly interpretable decision instrument (paper23 Table 2).
- Use a defined token for structurally inapplicable cells - 'NA, not applicable' - for single-arm-only/arm-inapplicable rows (control arm has no groin puncture/mTICI), never a blank or zero; use a dash to mark reference/not-applicable cells and to document which variables were dropped from the multivariable step; use '...' to separate descriptive rows from 'Measure of effect' rows (paper08 'MEVO 31 - -'; paper11 Table 2 blank multivariate cells; paper19; paper20; paper26 Table 2 / Table 1 footnote 'NA, not applicable').
- Give full interpretive glosses for QoL/disability instruments including unusual anchors ('The EQ-5D-5L ... Scores range from -0.39 to 1.00 ... 0 is the value of a health state equivalent to death, negative values are worse than death'), name death as an mRS level ('6, death'), and resolve domain edge-cases (occlusion-segment anomalies, derived-variable definitions, single-patient drug anomalies) citing the data source for derived/composite variables ('Number of occlusion sites: Sum of the number of occlusion sites ... according to the images'; 'The cause of stroke was evaluated based on the medical history, clinical characteristics, and imaging results') (paper20 Table 2 EQ-5D-5L/Barthel; paper22 Table 1/2; paper23 Table 1; paper24 Table 1; paper26 Table 1 footnotes † and §). [This is the disability-instrument specialization of the scale-regloss doctrine; canonical statement in figure_legends.moves.]
- Encode the model-selection / analytic narrative in the table BODY rather than the footnote: show reference levels as a dash with 0 points (or a literal '1' for the OR reference), mark dropped variables with '-', stage univariate vs multivariate columns, split into Model 1 / Model 2 sub-headers to keep collinear predictors out of the same model, and let the numeric format in the row label signal the implied test ('mean (SD)' -> t-test, 'median (IQR)' -> Mann-Whitney) - the table tells the selection story without prose, with the rationale living in Results not the footnote (paper03 Table 1; paper04 Table 2; paper08; paper12 Table 3; paper13 Table 4).
- Park the full operating-characteristic package and cut-off-selection grid in the table while arguing only the chosen threshold in prose: each cut-off row carries Sens/Spec/PPV/NPV each with its own 95% CI plus AUC; define composite/model predictors as explicit formulas at point of use ('Model_1, recanalization + NIHSSpre; Model_2, recanalization + NIHSSpre + post-treatment FVH'); disambiguate units and any parenthetical CI so a bracketed 95% CI is not misread as an IQR ('The numbers in the parentheses are the 95% confidence interval') (paper12 Table 4; paper13 Table 4; paper11 Table 3).
- Append laterality/qualifier clarifications to imaging-variable abbreviations in the footnote ('MTT, mean transit time, qualifying side; rCBF, relative cerebral blood flow, qualifying/contralateral side.') (paper04 Tables 1-2; paper06 Table 1).
- PROVISIONAL / LOW-N journal house-style punctuation split (each pattern drawn from only 1-2 papers per journal in a ~21-paper sample; treat as a starting hypothesis and verify against the target journal's CURRENT author instructions, not as a settled rule): JAHA-style footnotes tend to use 'TERM indicates expansion; and TERM, expansion.' (paper05/paper06/paper09/paper26), NEJM/JAMA idiom uses *, †, ‡, §, ¶, ‖ symbol keys (paper21/paper22), and Translational Stroke Research / European Radiology footnotes tend to use italic-run-on 'TERM expansion,' without 'indicates' (paper07/paper08). Match whatever the target journal actually requires; expand exhaustively either way.
- Body-level conventions that make tables self-contained and say what NOT to conclude: print the endpoint cut-off inside the table (paper05 Table 2 row 'Modified Rankin scale score >2'; paper26 Table 2 stubs 'modified Rankin Scale score at 90 d, 0-2 / 0-1 / 0-3'), keep null/non-significant rows in the table rather than pruning them so the display item itself states the limit of inference (paper05 central negative row P=0.40 unflagged; paper07 Table 2 null NOE# row; paper11 Table 2 FIV/HT/good-outcome all p>0.05), show non-estimable OR as an ellipsis on a 0-event row while still giving P (paper05 'Rehemorrhage 0 (0) 23 (8.8) ... 0.027*'), and structure each outcome row as raw n/N (%) FIRST then a labeled 'Measure of effect' sub-row with the outcome-appropriate named estimate (paper19 Table 2; paper20 'Measure of effect' column).

## What to offload to display items vs main text

- Two-table economy for a focused single-centre study (paper01): offload ALL baseline/comorbidity data and ALL procedural+safety outcomes onto exactly two tables; reserve the main text for interpretation. The MAIN TEXT states the clinical conclusion and cherry-picks decision-relevant numbers; the DISPLAY ITEM holds the exact n (%), IQR, OR, 95% CI, p, and Sens/Spec/PPV/NPV. Tables carry the audit trail; text carries the clinical argument. Verbatim: "The distribution of comorbidities was evenly balanced between the groups (Table 2)."; paper12 "Detailed clinical information of our patient cohort was shown in Table 1".
- Assign each display item a DISTINCT clinical-decision job and let the prose narrate only the headline. Recurring decision-centered division of labor across genres: Table 1 = who the patients were / device / dose / comparability; an evaluation table = is the image good enough to trust and is the finding visible (paper09 Table 2, whose head-to-head image-quality / diagnostic-confidence comparison was paper09's prespecified PRIMARY endpoint - the imaging-technique genre); the outcome table = the answer / what predicts a bad result (paper11 Table 2, paper12 Table 3, paper13 Tables 3-4). paper05 sequence: Fig 1 = what the disease looks like; Fig 2 = how the cohort was built; Fig 3 = whether matching is trustworthy; Table 1 = baseline balance before/after; Table 2 = what happened; Table 3 = what predicts a bad result.
- The standard RCT main-text quartet/quintet: (1) CONSORT/PRISMA flow diagram = auditable cohort accounting; (2) baseline + procedural characteristics table = who/comparability; (3) primary-endpoint mRS distribution stacked bar (or efficacy table) = the full ordinal answer; (4) prespecified subgroup forest plot = effect-modification for treatment selection; plus a combined Primary->Secondary->Safety outcomes table. This recurs across paper22, paper24, paper25 (with PRISMA in paper23). paper24/paper25 keep the main set deliberately small (3 figures + 3 tables; 4 display items).
- Make the table the validity argument when matching is used: show an explicit BEFORE/AFTER balance demonstration (unmatched beside matched, with the asterisk visibly disappearing after matching, e.g. 'Sex, female ... <0.001* ... 1.00'), show exactly the covariates entered into the PS model, and quantify balance with SMD rather than baseline P; pair it with a PS-overlap figure whose legend states the verdict ('substantial overlap ... large area of common support') (paper01 Table 1; paper05 Fig 3 + Table 1; paper26 Table 1).
- MECHANISM and biomarker data are kept OUT of the main display items entirely - pushed to prose and appendix so figures/tables stay patient-outcome-centred. paper09 keeps detector physics (cadmium telluride, photon counting) in main text only; paper14 offloads blood-occludin/BBB mechanism to prose+appendix; there is no biochemistry/mechanism figure in any exemplar. The display items carry the confirmatory/hierarchy burden with patient-centred, decision-relevant endpoints only. (For paper09 specifically, the imaging-technique genre means image quality / diagnostic confidence is the headline display burden and detector physics is the offloaded mechanism.)
- Choose a distribution/stacked-bar figure to convey the full ordinal mRS spectrum (including death/mRS 6) that a single dichotomized number cannot - a patient-centred choice - and report mRS BOTH as dichotomized clinical cut-points (in the outcome table) AND as an ordinal SHIFT distribution (in a figure), giving the table for decision thresholds and the figure for the full shift (paper19 Fig 2; paper20 Fig 1; paper21 Fig 2; paper26 Table 2 + Fig 2).
- Build the main table as a bedside tool when the paper IS a clinical instrument (paper03): an additive predictor block (status levels, model coefficient, integer 'Points assigned', reference level as a dash with 0 points) plus a second block cross-tabulating total points against patients, restenoses, and observed restenosis rate (monotonic 0% -> 75.0%); pair it with a worked-case figure (paper03 Fig 3) that maps each radiologic finding to a scored predictor, converting a 'representative image' into decision support.
- Argue the chosen operating point in prose but PARK the alternatives in the table: the cut-off-selection grid (multiple thresholds each with full Sens/Spec/PPV/NPV +/- CI and AUC) lives in a table while only the selected cut-off is defended in Results; use table STRUCTURE (Model 1/Model 2 sub-headers, dashes) to encode analytic decisions (keeping collinear predictors apart) with the rationale in Results not the footnote (paper12 Table 4 + Table 3; paper13 Table 4).
- Show how each association behaves after adjustment with parallel columns and show ROBUSTNESS as a design feature, not an assertion: place both estimation strategies side-by-side each with its own P ('Unadjusted OR | Unadjusted P | Adjusted OR | Adjusted P'; 'IPTW-adjusted Effect*' beside 'IVA-adjusted Effect*'), and structure each outcome row as raw n/N (%) FIRST then a labeled 'Measure of effect' sub-row with the outcome-appropriate named estimate, so the reader sees the absolute event rate before the relative estimate (paper02 Table 2; paper08 Table 3; paper19 Table 2; paper20 Table 2).
- Use the display item to flag what NOT to conclude - keep negatives where the decision can see them: retain null/non-significant rows in tables rather than pruning (paper07 Table 2 null NOE# row; paper05 central negative P=0.40 unflagged; paper11 Table 2 FIV/HT/good-outcome all p>0.05; paper08 explicit null subgroups in the forest plot), flag the null comparison explicitly in the figure legend (paper12 Fig 4 HT row - see the canonical null-flag move in figure_legends.moves), show discordant P values and CIs that cross 1 rather than hiding them (paper19 sICH 11.01 [1.37, 88.26] IPTW vs 10.41 [0.92, 118.16] IVA; mRS 0-1 IPTW P=.07 next to IVA P=.047), and refuse to cherry-pick inside the item - keep the honest trade-off that cuts against the thesis in the SAME table (paper09 Table 2 reports higher SNR/CNR for the comparator alongside the favorable accuracy metrics - and because image quality/diagnostic confidence was paper09's PRIMARY endpoint, those SNR/CNR rows are part of the headline result, not a buried caveat).
- Build the flow figure as the trial's case-selection logic: carry clinical, eligibility-coded exclusion reasons (age, time window, NIHSS range, ASPECTS/core/artery, prestroke mRS) mirroring the inclusion criteria, with exact numbers, so generalizability is auditable and the screening-to-inclusion funnel stays out of the running text (paper21 Fig 1; paper24 Fig 1; paper25 Fig 1 mirrors the funnel; paper02 Fig 1 flow-chart legend mostly expands exclusion-reason abbreviations; paper26 Fig 1 absorbs 991->315).
- Organize outcome tables around the prespecified OUTCOME HIERARCHY with explicit in-table section headers (Primary -> Secondary -> Safety / NIHSS) that mirror the Abstract and Results ordering, and group baseline tables by clinically meaningful blocks (Demographics / Clinical / Procedural) carrying units/time windows in the row labels so each number is self-describing; co-locate process/quality transportability metrics (recanalization, first-pass effect, mTICI 2b/3) in a named procedural sub-block ('Details of operative treatment') rather than scattering in prose (paper19 Table 2; paper20 Table 2; paper21 Table 2; paper24; paper26 Table 1/2).
- Tier content explicitly to the SUPPLEMENT to keep main displays focused on the primary clinical question, but cite each item by name in legends/text and preserve the honest counter-example locatably: push univariate/per-protocol/unadjusted analyses, sensitivity/missing-data/reclassification analyses, IPTW/ITT/worst-best-case imputation, additional adverse events, causes of death, vital signs, arterial-blood-gas, secondary within-subgroup multivariable models, biomarker/mechanism data, and the discordant/edge cases (paper04 Tables S1-S2; paper06 Tables S1-S2 with cited covariate provenance; paper07 Supplementary Table 1 + Figs S1-S2; paper08 Supplemental Table 1; paper09 'Table S1', 'the only discordant case ... Figure S2'; paper22 eTables/eFigures; paper24 appendix pp; paper25 8 eTables + 3 eFigures; paper26 Tables S1-S15 robustness apparatus).
- Offload an entire secondary analysis to a display item the text merely references (a time-distribution table carrying the whole FVH-prevalence-by-EVT-to-MRI-interval analysis the text does not tabulate; a subgroup reclassification analysis) (paper13 Table 5; paper11).
- Use a dedicated display item to do TRUST/comparability work a basic-science paper would not need (PS-overlap figure + before/after balance table whose legend states the verdict), and mirror a dual objective 1:1 across the display layer so each aim has its own evidence carrier (paper05 Fig 3 + Table 1; paper07 image-quality evidence Figs 2-5 vs prognostic-association evidence Table 2; paper14 ROC + regression table). For an imaging-TECHNIQUE paper (paper09) the dual carriers are the image-quality/diagnostic-confidence evaluation (PRIMARY) and the diagnostic-accuracy table (SECONDARY) - keep that primary/secondary ordering in the display layer.
- Make EVERY display item self-contained and self-decoding via three-tier abbreviation scope (the per-item abbreviation-key doctrine; canonical statement in figure_legends.moves): a paper-wide boxed Abbreviations glossary (top tier), a per-table footnote glossary, and a full glossary at the end of representative-figure legends - each scoped to its own item - so any table or figure read in isolation is fully interpretable; name the analysis population in the title, re-gloss every scale's range+direction (per the scale-regloss doctrine in figure_legends.moves), and put the endpoint definition with the number (paper11; paper13; paper19 boxed glossary + per-table + per-figure; paper21; paper22; paper24; paper25).
- Enforce a strict DIVISION OF LABOUR: statistical defensiveness (multiplicity caveats, missing-data accounting, assumption checks, method-switches, adjustment sets, p-sidedness) lives in Methods and in table/figure footnotes, leaving the Results/Discussion prose confident and patient-centred; the footnotes carry the caveats so the narrative does not have to (named signature of paper24 and paper25).
- Reference style for in-text calls: the display item is EVIDENCE for a sentence, never the grammatical subject - lead with the clinical claim and trail a terse parenthetical pointer that distinguishes main exhibits from supplement, bundling appendix page ranges ('(Table 2)', '(Figure 1)', '(Fig. 2, table 2; appendix pp 33-34)', '...is shown in eTable 5 in Supplement 2'). Use content-describing active verbs ('Table 1 presents...', 'Table 2 details...', 'as illustrated in Figure 2', 'Figure 1B illustrates...') and bind illustrative figures to the quantitative table row they exemplify (paper01 '...required access switching... (Fig 2)' bound to the 'Converted to alternative access' row). Where the interpretation is the point, weave the pointer into the clinical sentence ('This forest plot shows that the difference in the primary outcome ... favored the EVT group across all subgroups (Figure 3)'). The take-home claim lives in the figure legend, not the Results sentence. PROVISIONAL / LOW-N house-style note (verify against the target journal's current author instructions): BMJ journals tend to use lowercase 'figure'/'table', while Academic Radiology / IJS / NEJM use 'Fig'/'Figure'/'Table' (paper01; paper02; paper05; paper08; paper14; paper20; paper21; paper23; paper24; paper25; paper26).
- Keep endpoint definitions and cut-offs in Methods for a focused single-centre paper (table rows use bare labels and inherit Methods-defined cut-offs/instrument ranges) EXCEPT when a table must stand alone for a broad/RCT audience, in which case put the exposure's operational definition and the scale's range+direction into the footnote (the scale-regloss doctrine; canonical statement in figure_legends.moves); the endpoint and its cut-off (e.g., mRS 0-2 = good outcome) are defined ONCE in Methods/Abstract then referenced by compact row labels carrying the band, with the actionable scale anchors (Likert/confidence levels) living as ROW LABELS inside the table body when the table's job is image quality (paper01; paper04; paper09 Table 2 'Diagnostic confidence for in-stent patency' with each level a row - the image-quality genre; paper13 'Good outcome at 3 months, n (%)'; paper26 Table 2 stubs).
- Project-specific calibration (CLAUDE.md): see the canonical 'prefer-shorter / paper23 calibration' note in figure_legends.pitfalls - the Lancet-IPD exemplar (paper23) sits at the statistically dense end of footnoting (full adjustment set, p-sidedness, per-cell missingness, collinearity exclusions). For this skill, prefer SHORTER, concept-focused legends/footnotes and confident, non-defensive main text - borrow the decision-relevant elements (endpoint definition, cut-off, named classification, direction anchors, adjustment intent, population tag) but NOT paper23's defensive per-cell density; emulate paper22/24/25's patient-facing self-contained items over exhaustive per-cell statistical disclosure where a choice must be made.
