# Section-by-Section Clinical Writing Playbook

Distilled from 14 exemplar papers of a neurovascular/stroke + neuroimaging group (corresponding authors Sheng Liu, Hai-Bin Shi, Fei-Yun Wu; journals incl. JAHA, Translational Stroke Research, European Radiology, Academic Radiology, JNIS). Every exemplar sentence is quoted **verbatim** and tagged with the source paper (paperNN).

Use this when drafting or rewriting a specific section. For each section: do the *clinical moves*, avoid the *basic-science pitfalls*, and model the *exemplar sentences*. `variant notes` tell you how the moves differ between **interventional/cohort/prognostic** papers and **imaging/diagnostic-accuracy** papers.

## Title

**Goal:** Signal a clinical decision/prognostic/diagnostic question, the named patient population, and (often) the study design or reference standard at a glance — never a mechanism or a piece of technology for its own sake.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Lead with the clinical endpoint, predictor, or comparison ('Clinical Outcomes of X Versus Y'; '[Biomarker] as a predictor of [outcome]'; 'Incidence and predictors of...').
- For imaging/prognostic-biomarker papers use the high-frequency 'Predictors of [outcome]...', 'Prognostic value of [imaging marker] for/in [outcome] ... after [procedure]', or '[marker] predicts [outcome]' template (paper07, paper10-14).
- For diagnostic-accuracy papers, name BOTH the technology AND the clinical task it serves ('... for Noninvasive Detection of Neurovascular In-Stent Restenosis'), keeping the patient-management application front and center rather than the detector physics (paper09).
- When the paper ranks two markers, signal the head-to-head clinical comparison in the title ('comparison with infarction volume'; 'outperforms target mismatch') (paper12).
- Name the patient population in disease terms (acute ischemic stroke, symptomatic artery occlusion, non-acute internal carotid artery occlusion).
- Frequently append the study design / parent trial as a subtitle to flag rigor ('A Propensity Score-Matched Analysis', 'a post-hoc analysis of the CMOSS trial', 'results of DIRECT-MT trial', 'A Multicenter Observational Study'). OPTIONAL, not mandatory: imaging/biomarker titles (paper07, paper09, paper11-14) and some predictor titles (paper02) omit it, and a case count may stand in for it (paper03, 'in 252 cases').

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Foregrounding the technology/physics or molecule rather than the clinical task (write 'for Noninvasive Detection of ... In-Stent Restenosis', not the detector name alone).
- Mechanism-first or pathway-novelty phrasing.
- Hype words / 'first-ever' framing in the title.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- Comparing Transbrachial and Transradial as Alternatives to Transfemoral Access for Large-Bore Neuro Stenting: Insights From a Propensity-Matched Study (paper01)
- Ultra-High-Resolution Photon-Counting CT Angiography for Noninvasive Detection of Neurovascular In-Stent Restenosis (paper09)
- Predictors of malignant middle cerebral artery infarction after endovascular thrombectomy: results of DIRECT-MT trial (paper10)
- Prognostic value of ASPECTS on post-treatment diffusion-weighted imaging for acute ischemic stroke patients after endovascular thrombectomy: comparison with infarction volume (paper12)
- ASPECTS-based net water uptake predicts poor reperfusion and poor clinical outcomes in patients with ischemic stroke (paper14)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: 'X Versus Y', 'Incidence and predictors of...', design subtitle frequent. Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): name technology + clinical task ('for Noninvasive Detection of...'); reference standard often implicit. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): 'Prognostic value of [marker] for [outcome] after [procedure]' or declarative '[marker] predicts [outcome]'; parent trial appended only for RCT secondary analyses (paper10).

## Abstract

**Goal:** State a decision-relevant objective, anchor to a real-world cohort, foreground effect sizes on hard clinical endpoints AND (for tests) diagnostic-performance metrics with a reference standard, and land on an actionable, hedged recommendation; populate the journal's specific structured boxes.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Open Background/Objective on the clinical complication, dilemma, or unmet question and its severity ('common and severe complication'; 'remains unclear'; 'remains challenging'), then state the aim tied to a decision point.
- For Eur Radiol / TSR / imaging papers, open the structured Objective with a BARE INFINITIVE: 'To investigate/assess/explore/determine/evaluate the (prognostic/diagnostic) value of [marker] for [clinical endpoint]' (paper07, paper09, paper10-14). This 'To investigate the value of...' infinitive is canonical clinical voice, NOT a mechanism flag.
- Describe Methods in clinical-cohort terms: retrospective/prospective consecutive cohort at a named center, named multicenter trial, or post-hoc RCT with registration number; enrollment window, sample size, matched-cohort numbers, the OUTCOME DEFINITION up front (e.g. 'Good outcome was defined as a 3-month mRS 0-2'), and — for tests — the REFERENCE STANDARD (DSA; follow-up DWI/FIV; ASL) and the index test.
- Lead Results by study type. PROGNOSTIC/PREDICTOR: absolute event rate/prevalence (48/395, 12.2%; GIC in 15.7%), then adjusted OR/HR with 95% CI and exact P; for markers, ROC discrimination (AUC with 95% CI, sensitivity, specificity, cut-off). DIAGNOSTIC-ACCURACY (paper09): lead with subjective image-quality / diagnostic-confidence superiority and the raw sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV/accuracy vs the comparator — NOT an AUC/cut-off. PROCEDURAL-COMPARATIVE: technical/clinical success and procedural-time/patient-experience contrasts (% / median + P).
- Land the Conclusion on a single actionable independent-predictor statement, a pragmatic ranking of the alternative, or the marker's role for early risk identification — hedged where evidence is limited ('may be an effective prognostic marker').
- Populate the JOURNAL-SPECIFIC structured box: JAHA 'What is new / What are the clinical implications'; Eur Radiol papers (07, 10-14) a bulleted 'Key Points' box (distilled as absolute risk + prognostic impact + actionable predictor/cut-off), with a separate 'Clinical relevance' statement seen in paper07. paper09 is published in Stroke (AHA): it carries Key Words and a graphic abstract but has NO Key Points and NO Clinical relevance box — do NOT fabricate one for a diagnostic-accuracy/Stroke paper.

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- An Objective phrased as 'to investigate the MECHANISM/biological role of' rather than 'to evaluate/predict/assess the value of [marker] for [endpoint]'.
- Reporting only P values without absolute rates, CIs, effect sizes, or (for a test) sensitivity/specificity.
- Omitting the reference standard or the outcome definition in a diagnostic/prognostic abstract.
- Forcing a Key Points/Clinical relevance box onto a Stroke/AHA or JAHA diagnostic paper, or reporting an AUC/Youden cut-off for a binary diagnostic-accuracy read that has none.
- Concluding about biological understanding instead of a management implication; overclaiming beyond a single-center/post-hoc/retrospective design.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- To investigate the value of automated Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score (ASPECTS)-based net water uptake (NWU) to predict tissue-level reperfusion status and 90-day functional outcomes in acute ischemic stroke (AIS) patients after reperfusion therapy. (paper14)
- Of the 395 enrolled patients, 48 (12.2%) had sICH. (paper02)
- UHR PCD-CTA achieved excellent diagnostic performance for ISR detection, with a sensitivity of 100.0%, specificity of 97.3%, and overall accuracy of 98.0%. (paper09)
- Setting post-treatment DWI ASPECTS ≥ 6 as a cut-off value, optimal performance (AUC = 0.836; sensitivity, 87.1%; specificity, 66.7%) could be obtained in predicting good clinical outcome, which was comparable with that of infarction volume (cut-off volume, ≤ 94.87 ml; AUC = 0.821; sensitivity, 90.3%; specificity, 55.6%). (paper12)
- Post-treatment FVH may be an effective prognostic marker associated with clinical outcome in patients with AIS after EVT. (paper13)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: objective may be a full sentence ('This study aimed to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of...'); lead Results with event rate + OR/HR. Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): objective = 'evaluate the diagnostic performance of [index test] ... compared with [reference standard]'; lead Results with image-quality/diagnostic-confidence + raw sensitivity/specificity/accuracy; NO Key Points box (Stroke journal). Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): bare-infinitive objective + define outcome AND reference standard (DWI/FIV, ASL) up front + report AUC/cut-off + bulleted Key Points box (Eur Radiol).

## Introduction

**Goal:** Funnel from disease burden / standard of care (or the limit/residual-burden of current standard-of-care imaging or treatment) to an explicit, decision-centered clinical gap and a single clear aim, with the confounding-control or reference-standard rationale stated.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Open from disease burden / epidemiology / standard of care (proportion of strokes attributable, population incidence, recurrence risk despite best therapy, the traditional default procedure, or the ~80% mortality that defines a malignant phenotype). For imaging-biomarker papers, open from the RESIDUAL burden despite proven treatment (~50% still not functionally independent after EVT) or the LIMITATION of current standard-of-care imaging that motivates the new marker.
- Escalate the clinical stakes: residual risk despite optimal medical management, downstream harms, a clinical pressure driving change, or an actionable lever (decompressive craniectomy works better before herniation, so early identification matters).
- For diagnostic/imaging papers, run the textbook diagnostic funnel: clinical complication and its timeline -> limits of the invasive reference standard (DSA risk) -> limits of the noninvasive alternative (CTA blooming artifact; manual volumetry slow; automated software unreliable/unavailable) -> residual unmet need -> the candidate marker (often validated in an adjacent domain such as coronary) -> the precise gap (not yet reported in neurovascular / not compared with the reference).
- Situate the work in the current treatment landscape and cite pivotal trials (incl. negative ones: COSS/CMOSS/SAMMPRIS; the parent trial DIRECT-MT) to frame why subgroups or markers must be found.
- State the unmet need explicitly as a clinical/evidence/practice gap ('studies ... are lacking', 'remains unclear', 'no publications have reported', 'still not clear until now', 'few studies ... had been reported', 'is urgently needed'), optionally with a measured 'To our knowledge, this is the first...'.
- Close with an explicit aim (or numbered aims) naming the real-world cohort and reference standard, plus the confounding-control rationale when PSM is used, or the surrogacy/comparison framing when ranking two markers.

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Opening from a molecular/mechanistic knowledge gap instead of disease burden, standard of care, residual treatment burden, or a standard-of-care imaging limitation.
- Framing the aim as understanding biology rather than informing a clinical decision.
- Multiple diffuse aims; failing to funnel to one decision-centered question (numbered aims are fine if each is decision-relevant).
- Over-hyped novelty claims beyond a single restrained 'first study' statement.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- Hemorrhagic transformation (HT), especially symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage (sICH), is a common and fatal complication in patients with acute ischemic stroke (AIS) after endovascular thrombectomy (EVT), which decreases or counteracts the benefit of thrombectomy. (paper02)
- Due to a reported mortality rate of approximately 80%, this subgroup of MCA stroke has been labeled as malignant MCA infarction (mMCAi). (paper10)
- Digital subtraction angiography (DSA) remains the reference standard for surveillance of ISR; however, its invasive nature is associated with a small but definite risk of periprocedural complications. (paper09)
- Therefore, a simple, accessible, and reproducible imaging marker with the potential to predict the prognosis of patients with AIS after EVT is urgently needed. (paper13)
- Therefore, the purpose of the present study was to explore the predictors of GIC on baseline CTP in stroke patients achieving successful recanalization after MT, using follow-up DWI as the reference standard. (paper11)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: burden -> residual risk despite therapy -> negative-trial framing -> PSM rationale (paper01-06,08). Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): full diagnostic funnel ending in 'not yet reported in neurovascular' + aim to evaluate diagnostic performance vs DSA. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): open from residual post-treatment burden or imaging limitation -> name the reference standard (FIV/DWI, ASL) and the surrogacy/comparison gap -> numbered aims are common.

## Methods

**Goal:** Establish a reproducible real-world clinical cohort with ethics governance, standardized definitions, validated scales, a named reference standard with blinded reads, and a prespecified confounding-control / diagnostic-accuracy / imaging-statistics plan.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Lead with the real-world setting and ethics: retrospective analysis of a prospectively maintained registry / consecutive patients at a named comprehensive/tertiary center, named multicenter cohort, or post-hoc analysis of a named RCT; IRB approval (number), Declaration of Helsinki/GCP, informed consent or waiver.
- Give explicit numbered clinical inclusion/exclusion criteria in operational terms (age range, stenosis thresholds, occlusion site, time windows, baseline/pre-stroke mRS, required imaging availability), with a CONSORT-style accounting from screened to analyzed.
- Anchor every outcome and disease term to a recognized clinical classification or validated criteria (Heidelberg Bleeding Classification, NASCET, WASID, TICI/mTICI/eTICI, Hunt-Hess, modified Fisher, mRS dichotomized good/poor at 90 d, NIHSS, ASPECTS) and the cut-off (ISR = ≥50%; successful recanalization = mTICI 2b-3).
- For imaging/diagnostic papers, NAME THE REFERENCE STANDARD and the index test explicitly (DSA when clinically indicated; follow-up DWI/FIV; ASL for tissue-level reperfusion), and justify the reference-standard strategy pragmatically (a 2-tier reference standard to minimize work-up bias; DSA withheld in low-suspicion patients to avoid unnecessary invasive procedures). For a head-to-head index-test-vs-comparator design, describe the PAIRED same-patient comparison and reading-order controls (e.g. simulated EID-CTA reconstructed from the same raw data and read FIRST, with a 4-week washout to minimize recall bias; randomized case order), driving paired Wilcoxon/paired-t/McNemar within-subject testing.
- Use blinded, independent reads by stated-experience radiologists with consensus/third-reader adjudication for any subjective imaging/event read (sICH, restenosis, FVH/GIC presence, ASPECTS, perfusion maps, image quality), and REPORT INTER-READER REPRODUCIBILITY (weighted Cohen kappa or ICC with 95% CI, with a poor/fair/good/excellent band). Blinded adjudication is appropriately ABSENT for objective procedural-metric (paper01) or administrative-mortality (paper05) endpoints.
- Describe the real-world clinical workflow (access/treatment selection by stroke team, antiplatelet regimens, follow-up schedule, endpoint ascertainment via records/imaging core lab or stroke-nurse-specialist interview).
- State the statistical plan in clinically interpretable terms: univariate screen (P<0.10 or <0.05) feeding multivariable logistic/Cox regression, OR/HR with 95% CI; ROC with Youden-index cut-off, AUC, sensitivity/specificity (prognostic/predictor papers); the named DeLong test when comparing two markers' AUCs (paper12, paper13); calibration via Hosmer-Lemeshow ONLY where a single combined prediction model is the deliverable (paper10) — it is not a default; for DIAGNOSTIC-ACCURACY, sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV with raw fractions vs the comparator (NO ROC/Youden/AUC for a binary read); PSM (logistic-regression PS, caliper, named ratio) with balance diagnostics (SMD/variance ratio) for matched comparisons; Kaplan-Meier/log-rank for time-to-event. IMAGING-STATISTICS sub-plan when markers are correlated: run collinearity diagnostics (VIF, threshold >10 or graded 1-5-10) and either split correlated markers into PARALLEL multivariable models (paper12) or switch to STEPWISE selection (paper11, paper14); relate two imaging measures with a Spearman/Pearson correlation coefficient reported against a slight/fair/moderate/good/excellent band (paper11, paper12); visualize method-comparison against the reference with a Bland-Altman mean difference + limits of agreement (paper11). Declare reporting-guideline adherence — STROBE/STROCSS (the diagnostic-accuracy exemplar paper09 declares STROBE, NOT STARD; STARD does not appear in the corpus and should not be asserted as observed practice) — and an a priori power/sample-size calculation where applicable (paper09's was sized on the PRIMARY image-quality endpoint via a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test); report radiation dose (CTDIvol, DLP) for CT procedures. For Eur Radiol submissions (paper07, paper10-14) emit the journal's structural declaration block: a bulleted 'Methodology' self-label (e.g. '• retrospective • case-control study • performed at one institution' for paper11-14; '• Prospective • Observational' for paper07), plus 'Guarantor', 'Statistics and biometry', 'Informed consent', and 'Ethical approval' declarations.

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Describing assays/protocols without human-subjects governance, cohort framing, or explicit eligibility criteria.
- Using ad hoc outcome definitions instead of validated clinical classifications and scales, or failing to name the reference standard for a test.
- Omitting the confounding-control plan / matching covariates, OR omitting inter-reader reproducibility and blinding for a subjective imaging read.
- Entering strongly correlated imaging markers into one multivariable model without a collinearity (VIF) check and the parallel-model / stepwise remedy.
- Asserting STARD adherence for a diagnostic paper (the corpus uses STROBE), or forcing ROC/Youden/AUC onto a binary diagnostic-accuracy read.
- Presenting a prognostic imaging marker without a prespecified ROC/cut-off plan tying it to a clinical decision.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- sICH was defined according to the Heidelberg Bleeding Classification. (paper02)
- To minimize work-up bias and clarify the adjudication process, a 2-tier reference standard was used. DSA served as the definitive reference when clinically indicated or when CTA raised a high suspicion of ISR. (paper09)
- Collinearity analysis was performed using a variance inflation factor (VIF) > 10 as the threshold value. If collinearity was indicated, a stepwise method was used in multivariate logistic regression analysis to avoid the influence of collinearity. (paper11)
- Due to strong correlation and potential collinearity, two multivariate logistic regression models were built, respectively. (paper12)
- Bland-Altman plots were used to illustrate the mean differences and limit of agreement (LoA) between the volume of CTP-estimated ischemic core and final infarct. (paper11)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: PSM covariates named + balance diagnostics; outcome adjudication by committee/nurse-specialist (paper01-06,08). Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): explicit reference standard + 2-tier strategy + work-up-bias control + paired same-patient comparison with 4-week washout + weighted kappa band + power calc on the image-quality primary endpoint + radiation dose + STROBE. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): index test vs reference standard (FIV/DWI, ASL); blinded multi-reader with ICC/kappa band; ROC/Youden cut-off plan; VIF/collinearity -> parallel-model or stepwise; correlation-with-band surrogacy; Bland-Altman; DeLong for two-marker comparison (paper12/13 only); Hosmer-Lemeshow only for the single combined model (paper10); Eur Radiol Methodology/Guarantor/Statistics block (self-labelled 'case-control study' for paper11-14 despite enrolling consecutive single-center retrospective patients). Cohort framing varies: 'consecutive patients' + named center for single-center registries (paper02/03/09/11/12/13/14); the randomized/multicenter parent-trial cohort + registration number for post-hoc RCT analyses (paper04/06/10); a named multicenter cohort otherwise (paper08).

## Results

**Goal:** Account for cohort flow, confirm matched-cohort balance / reader reproducibility, then present hard clinical endpoints and (for tests) diagnostic performance with absolute rates, effect sizes, CIs, cut-offs, and exact P values.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Open with patient-flow accounting (CONSORT-like): screened -> excluded with reasons -> analyzed, tied to a flow figure, plus a safety note where relevant ('All participants successfully underwent PCD-CTA without adverse events').
- Describe the cohort in clinician-recognizable summary statistics (median age, sex, NIHSS, ASPECTS, occlusion site, comorbidities) and confirm baseline balance after PSM using balance diagnostics (SMD/variance ratio); for imaging papers, report INTER-READER REPRODUCIBILITY (ICC or weighted kappa with 95% CI and band) BEFORE the associations.
- Lead with the absolute event rate / prevalence of the clinical phenomenon with its denominator (48/395, 12.2%; GIC in 18/115, 15.7%; 74/559, 13.2%; poor reperfusion in 40/112, 35.7%) before any inferential statistic. For an imaging-TECHNIQUE paper (paper09), it is legitimate to lead instead with the quantitative image-quality / diagnostic-confidence superiority (Likert scores, blooming, SNR/CNR) because that is the primary endpoint.
- Report primary clinical endpoints with group-by-group rates and exact P, foregrounding explicit 'vs.' contrasts with medians/IQRs/percentages; for procedural-comparative papers foreground technical/clinical success and procedural-time/patient-experience contrasts.
- Present adjusted associations as ORs/HRs with 95% CI for each clinically meaningful predictor, then translate the marker into a bedside cut-off with operating characteristics via ROC (Youden cut-off, AUC with 95% CI, sensitivity, specificity). When correlated imaging markers preclude a single model, report the VIF and the resulting parallel-model or stepwise solution (paper11: VIF 11.718 and 10.210 -> stepwise; paper12: rho=-0.847 -> two parallel models), and where two measures are compared for surrogacy report the correlation coefficient with its band (paper12). For DIAGNOSTIC-ACCURACY studies, report sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV/accuracy as raw numerators/denominators head-to-head against the (surrogate) comparator (paper09) — NOT an AUC/cut-off. When ranking two prognostic markers, report the named DeLong comparison ('no significant difference in AUC ... p = 0.686' paper12); note paper14 reports a nested-AUC gain (p=0.045) WITHOUT naming DeLong. Report combined-model calibration (Hosmer-Lemeshow) only where a single combined model is built (paper10).
- Add a practice-relevant subgroup or threshold analysis tied to a decision — and for this corpus the subgroup analysis often IS the clinical message: re-derive the rCBF core threshold stratified by onset-to-CTP time (paper11: <=207 min vs >207 min, rCBF<20% vs <30%) and conclude the threshold 'should be stratified by stroke onset time'; or stratify a successful-recanalization subgroup to expose 'futile recanalization' (paper14: n=64, 23.4% still poor reperfusion). Tie imaging findings to hard downstream endpoints (mMCAi -> 2.7% vs 41.9% functional independence; 64.9% vs 9.5% mortality).
- Explicitly flag non-significant / null findings rather than overclaiming ('no significant differences ... between the GIC and no-GIC group'; 'we found no evidence of a difference between groups'), and report any safety/harm signal co-equally with efficacy.

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Leading with mechanistic/secondary readouts instead of the primary clinical endpoint, event rate, diagnostic performance, or (for a technique paper) the prespecified image-quality primary endpoint.
- Reporting significance without absolute rates, denominators, 95% CIs, or (for a test) raw sensitivity/specificity fractions.
- Putting two strongly correlated imaging markers in one model and interpreting the resulting unstable ORs as findings, instead of reporting VIF and splitting/stepwise.
- Omitting inter-reader reproducibility for an imaging readout, or skipping patient-flow accounting and matched-cohort balance.
- Burying or omitting null/non-significant findings or the harm/safety signal.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- The median baseline D-­dimer levels were significantly higher in the patients with sICH than in the patients without sICH (median 2.70 vs 0.74 mg/L, P<0.001). (paper02)
- UHR PCD-CTA achieved excellent diagnostic performance for ISR detection, with a sensitivity of 100.0% (14/14), specificity of 97.3% (36/37), positive predictive value of 93.3% (14/15), negative predictive value of 100.0% (36/36), and overall accuracy of 98.0% (50/51; Table S1 and Figure 2). (paper09)
- Before logistic regression analysis, we first performed the collinearity analysis. As a result, we found that the VIF of hypoperfused area volume and mismatch area were 11.718 and 10.210, respectively. (paper11)
- The mean volumetric difference between CTP-estimated ischemic core and final infarct was −21.1 mL (LoA, −124.7 to 86 mL) (Fig. 1). (paper11)
- In a subgroup of patients who had successful recanalization after MT (n = 64), 49 of them (76.6%) showed good reperfusion on ASL, whereas 15 patients (23.4%) still exhibited poor reperfusion, indicating futile recanalization. (paper14)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: balance diagnostics after PSM; lead with event rate + OR/HR + cut-off (paper02-06,08); paper01 leads with success rates + procedural-time/patient-experience contrasts and reports no OR. Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): may lead with image-quality/diagnostic-confidence primary endpoint; report sensitivity/specificity/PPV/NPV/accuracy as raw fractions vs the surrogate comparator + weighted kappa band; no ROC/Youden/AUC. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): report ICC/kappa first; prevalence; OR + ROC cut-off; VIF -> parallel models (paper12) or stepwise (paper11/14); correlation-with-band surrogacy (paper11/12); Bland-Altman (paper11); DeLong for two-marker/two-model comparison (paper12/13; paper14 compares nested AUCs without naming DeLong); Hosmer-Lemeshow only for the combined model (paper10); onset-time threshold re-derivation and 'futile recanalization' subgroup are the headline message (paper11/14); flag null prognostic differences (paper11).

## Discussion

**Goal:** Restate the finding in clinical terms, benchmark against prior clinical evidence, keep mechanism subordinate, interpret the diagnostic/prognostic trade-offs in clinical terms, and land on an explicit clinical-implications/management paragraph.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Open by restating the main finding in clinical terms (success rates comparable; marker independently associated; subtype worse on hard endpoints; the test's accuracy), often as an enumerated list for predictor papers ('Our study demonstrated three main findings'), with a measured novelty claim where warranted ('To our knowledge, this is the first study to...').
- Benchmark results against named prior clinical series, registries, and RCTs, situating the work within the evidence hierarchy and reconciling discrepancies with clinical/practical factors (RCT rigor vs single-center; imaging protocol/threshold choices; patient constitution).
- For imaging/diagnostic papers, explain WHY the prior approach failed clinically (blooming/partial-volume underestimation; manual volumetry slow; software-dependent metrics not widely applicable; admission-only NWU less informative than dynamic ΔNWU), and interpret your own quantitative trade-offs HONESTLY as features, not defects: where a new modality is inferior on one metric (UHR PCD-CTA has LOWER SNR/CNR than EID-CTA), frame it as 'the expected trade-off between spatial resolution and' image noise, redeemed by better blooming reduction and diagnostic confidence; equal-weighted ASPECTS regions interpreted in terms of clinical interpretability.
- Offer biological/pathophysiological plausibility only as supportive mechanism for the clinical marker (coagulopathy, BBB disruption, no-reflow/microvascular thrombi, glucose-reperfusion injury, pH normalization, intimal hyperplasia), never as the central claim — subordinate it; an optional 'More importantly' (paper06, paper07) may elevate the patient-care message above the biological one.
- Include an explicit clinical-implications paragraph converting findings into management actions: surveillance intervals, antiplatelet adherence, limiting thrombectomy attempts, patient selection, how to INTERPRET a scan, and what NOT to do (do not exclude from MT on CTP core alone); for prognostic-imaging markers, frame the marker as facilitating early identification/management of high-risk patients and supporting individualized strategy.
- Address prior safety concerns / pose explicit risk-stratification questions ('which mild stroke patients are most likely to benefit from immediate EVT?'), and where a null is clinically reassuring, highlight it and corroborate with another named trial (IV alteplase before EVT not associated with mMCAi, corroborated by DEVT).

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Making mechanism the headline contribution rather than the clinical implication.
- Omitting benchmarking against named prior clinical cohorts/trials, or failing to explain why the prior imaging approach was clinically inadequate.
- Treating an honest quantitative trade-off (lower SNR/CNR) as a defeat instead of reframing it as the price of resolution / interpretability.
- Failing to translate the finding into a concrete management action or scan-interpretation instruction.
- Overclaiming causality from an observational/association design, or claiming test superiority beyond what the reference-standard/surrogate-comparator design supports.

**Exemplar sentences (verbatim from the corpus):**
- This finding would help in the early prediction of HT and perioperative management (such as limiting the number of thrombectomy attempts, determining the timing of procedure termination, and guiding the use of antithrombotic/anticoagulant agents). (paper02)
- More importantly, our data indicate that patients with symptomatic ICA or MCA occlusion who experienced a TIA at admission do not exhibit a lower risk of subsequent ipsilateral ischemic stroke compared with those presenting with a stroke. (paper06)
- Patients should not be excluded from recanalization therapy solely based on the ischemic core volume obtained from baseline CTP, especially in the early time window. (paper11)
- The cut-off value of ΔNWU > 2.12% in our study may be helpful for the early identification of patients with poor outcomes, which could guide clinicians to provide an individualized treatment strategy to mitigate brain edema and improve microvascular perfusion. (paper14)
- Taken together, these findings suggest that UHR PCD-CTA may represent a promising noninvasive approach for poststent surveillance. (paper09)

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: benchmark vs named registries/RCTs; clinical-implications paragraph on patient selection/perioperative management. Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): explain why conventional CTA fails (blooming), reframe the SNR/CNR trade-off as the expected price of resolution/interpretability, land on surveillance/risk-stratification role. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): often open with an enumerated 'main findings' list; argue the marker's practicality vs software-dependent/manual alternatives; deliver a scan-interpretation instruction and a cut-off for early risk stratification.

## Limitations

**Goal:** Acknowledge design-level threats pragmatically and in order, tie each to its clinical consequence, name biases with their direction (especially verification/selection bias for tests), counterbalance with a design strength where honest, and point to the next evidence step.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Lead with the design-level caveat (single-center retrospective / post-hoc / small event count / small cohort) and counsel cautious interpretation.
- Enumerate limitations in a measured, numbered list (First, Second, Third... / Firstly, Secondly...), framing each around its impact on clinical interpretation and the robustness of any derived tool or cut-off.
- Frame generalizability in real-world terms (single-ethnicity/Chinese cohort; threshold may differ across populations; single-software analysis invites external validation) and note clinically relevant variables the design omitted (stent type, CYP2C19, vasospasm, infarct topography).
- For imaging/diagnostic papers, name the specific bias and its DIRECTIONAL effect on the headline metrics: partial-verification/work-up bias may inflate specificity and NPV (paper09); selection bias because severe patients / those with symptomatic HT could not tolerate or undergo follow-up MRI (paper11, paper13, paper14); a simulated/surrogate comparator reconstructed from the same raw data limits superiority claims over real-world conventional CTA (paper09); and add a concrete power analysis where relevant (paper13).
- Counterbalance with a reassuring design strength where honest (large reference group, PSM balancing, multivariable adjustment, follow-up DWI as reference standard, blinded multi-reader). The 'Fortunately, we set a solid reference level...' move (paper02) is an OPTIONAL flourish.
- Land on a constructive call for stronger evidence — prospective cohorts, external validation against conventional systems, multicenter studies, and especially RCTs, sometimes citing ongoing trial registration numbers.

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Defensive, statistics-only caveats divorced from clinical consequence.
- Omitting the generalizability/population caveat or the missing clinically relevant variables.
- Failing to name biases and their direction — especially partial-verification/work-up bias for diagnostic-accuracy studies and MRI-tolerance selection bias for imaging-prognostic studies.
- Overstating that limitations are fully mitigated; no concrete next-step call for prospective/external validation.

**Variant notes (interventional/cohort vs imaging/diagnostic):** Interventional/cohort: single-center/post-hoc, small events, single-ethnicity threshold caveat, missing covariates (paper01-06,08). Diagnostic-accuracy (paper09): partial-verification bias inflating specificity/NPV; surrogate comparator reconstructed from the same raw data limiting superiority claims; cohort size/sex imbalance for generalizability across stent designs. Imaging-prognostic (paper10-14): MRI-tolerance selection bias (severe/HT patients excluded); single-software/single-center; concrete power analysis (paper13); call for automated/ML segmentation and prospective multicenter validation.

## Conclusion

**Goal:** Restate the bottom-line verdict in plain clinical language, deliver an actionable risk-stratified recommendation or scan-interpretation instruction, emphasize real-world practicality, and hedge to the level the evidence supports.

**Clinical moves (do these):**
- Restate the headline finding in plain, memorable clinical terms (a single independent-predictor sentence, a 'nearly one in five' statistic, or 'X% developed mMCAi after EVT').
- Name the actionable independent predictors / optimal-candidate subgroup / cut-off that should drive risk stratification or patient selection (separate confirmatory predictors from a novel one, e.g. pass number).
- Deliver a pragmatic, management-oriented recommendation: rank the alternative for first-line use; identify who benefits from a procedure; recommend a surveillance strategy; for prognostic-imaging markers, state the marker may facilitate early identification/management of high-risk patients; for a diagnostic test, state its role for noninvasive surveillance; and where warranted, a CAUTIONARY instruction ('Patients cannot be excluded from MT solely based on baseline CTP-derived ischemic core volume').
- Emphasize real-world practicality where relevant ('easily obtainable in clinical practice', 'simple and convenient clinical tool', 'simple, accessible, and reproducible', 'more practical for real clinical settings', 'beyond vascular imaging').
- Close with a measured, calibrated call for confirmatory evidence rather than an overclaim ('Further studies are warranted'; 'larger prospective studies ... are required'; 'further validation in larger cohorts and against conventional systems is warranted').

**Basic-science pitfalls (avoid these):**
- Concluding about biological understanding or mechanism rather than a management decision.
- Overclaiming from limited/retrospective/post-hoc/single-center evidence without a confirmatory caveat, or claiming a test is superior beyond the reference-standard/surrogate-comparator design.
- Vague significance language instead of a concrete, actionable recommendation or scan-interpretation instruction.
- Hype or 'first-ever' framing in the conclusion itself.
